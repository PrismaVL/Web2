In replacement of MK3, Toyota launched the Supra MK4 in 1993 with a more aerodynamic design. The new Supra had been in development for 4 years and was Toyota’s direct answer to the performance rivals offered by Mazda’s RX7 Turbo and Nissan’s 300ZX.
The MK4’s engine has become quite a legend as it’s offered in both naturally aspirated or twin-turbo forms. The 3-litre straight-six has 4 valves per cylinder, features sequential electronic fuel injection, an aluminum head, and a cast-iron block. The twin-turbo cars were fitted with an all-new 6-speed manual gearbox, whereas the naturally aspirated models had the W58 5 speed unit. Each model was also offered with a 4-speed automatic that featured a manual shift mode.
Toyota used aluminium for the hood, suspension arms, and transmission pans. Compared to the previous model, the MK4 was shorter, lower and wider, and 100kg lighter. Therefore, the car community quickly embraced the MK4 with numerous body tweaks.

Introduced in 1998 as the successor to the R33, the Nissan Skyline R34 arrived at a time when the automotive landscape was ripe with competition. From its inception, the R34 was destined for greatness, with each aspect meticulously crafted to elevate the driving experience to new heights. At its heart beat the legendary RB26DETT engine, a twin-turbocharged inline-six powerplant revered for its robustness and ability to unleash prodigious amounts of power with the faintest whisper of throttle input.

Introduced in 1999 to commemorate Honda's 50th anniversary, the S2000 was an instant sensation, captivating enthusiasts with its lightweight construction, front-engine, rear-wheel-drive layout, and perfect 50:50 weight distribution. At its heart lay a naturally aspirated 2.0-liter inline-four engine, renowned for its VTEC variable valve timing system and exhilarating redline of 9,000 RPM. With 240 horsepower on tap, the S2000 delivered blistering acceleration and an intoxicating exhaust note that begged to be explored on open roads and winding mountain passes.
